What is water footprint assessment?

Water footprint assessments can be conducted at various scales, from individual products or processes to entire supply chains, regions, or countries. They are valuable tools for identifying opportunities to reduce water consumption, improve water use efficiency, and minimize environmental impacts related to water use. Additionally, water footprint assessments can help raise awareness about the interconnectedness of water resources, consumption patterns, and environmental sustainability.

A water footprint assessment typically involves several phases:

1. Goal and scope definition: This phase involves determining the objectives of the assessment, the boundaries of the study (including the spatial and temporal boundaries), and the specific aspects of water use to be evaluated.

2. Inventory analysis: In this phase, data on water use and consumption throughout the product's life cycle or within a specific system are collected and quantified. This includes direct water use (e.g., irrigation, manufacturing processes) as well as indirect water use (e.g., water embedded in products or services).

3. Impact assessment: Here, the potential environmental impacts associated with water use are assessed. This may involve evaluating factors such as water scarcity, pollution, and ecosystem degradation, among others. Various indicators and methods can be used to quantify these impacts.

4. Reporting: Finally, the findings of the assessment are typically compiled into a report or other communication format to be shared with stakeholders, decision-makers, and the public. Clear and transparent reporting is essential for ensuring the credibility and usefulness of the assessment results.

    The purpose of a water footprint assessment is to:

  • Understand Water Usage: Determine how much water is being used in different stages of production, from manufacturing to distribution, and identify areas where water consumption can be reduced or optimized.
  • Identify Hotspots: Identify "hotspots" in the supply chain or production process where water consumption or pollution is particularly high, allowing for targeted interventions to reduce environmental impact.
  • Inform Decision-Making: Provide stakeholders with information to make more sustainable choices regarding water management, such as selecting water-efficient technologies, sourcing materials from less water-intensive suppliers, or implementing water reuse and recycling practices.
  • Raise Awareness: Increase awareness among consumers, businesses, and policymakers about the water footprint associated with different products and activities, fostering a greater understanding of the water resources required to support human activities and life.
